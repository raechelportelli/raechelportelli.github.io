Venezuela · what to watch forAn emptying middle

  1. A narrowing base from the mid-2010s onward as fertility falls.
  2. Cohort losses among working-age adults: more than seven million Venezuelans have left the country since 2014.
  3. Sex asymmetries in young-adult cohorts that may reflect gendered migration patterns.
  4. A pyramid that begins to invert by mid-century in the projections.

Haiti · what to watch forA still-broad base

  1. A wide base that narrows more slowly than Venezuela's, reflecting persistently higher fertility.
  2. The 2010 earthquake cohort: look for a visible dip in the youngest age groups in pyramids from 2015 onward.
  3. A thin upper apex shaped by lower life expectancy and out-migration of older adults.
  4. Projections show a still-young population at 2050, contrasting sharply with Venezuela.

Discussion prompts for the pairing

  1. Both countries sit on the Caribbean rim and share colonial-era histories of plantation economies, yet their pyramids diverge sharply. What does the shape of each pyramid suggest about the different paths their demographic transitions have taken?
  2. Where can you see events written into the chart? Compare a slow process (sustained fertility decline) with a sudden shock (an earthquake, an economic collapse, a migration wave).
  3. What assumptions go into the UN projections that drive years past 2025? Whose futures get smoothed over when we extrapolate?
  4. The pyramid encodes binary sex, five-year age bins, and the nation-state as the unit of analysis. What demographic realities (undocumented migrants, displaced persons, non-binary populations, intra-national inequality) does that frame make harder to see?
  5. If you were advising a Ministry of Health or Education in either country in 2030, what would each pyramid tell you to plan for?
